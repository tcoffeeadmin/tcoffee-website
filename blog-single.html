dear design.

A copywriting guide for designers and creatives.

  • james bearne
  • 10th may 2017

As usual, I’ve left writing Christmas cards until the last minute. The chore of playing catch up gets in the way of what’s nice, putting pen to paper.

As a Creative Director, I write a lot. Whether it’s for proposals, clients, or anything else around the agency, each day involves some form of creative writing. I actually enjoy writing. But I’m not a writer by trade. Far from it, in fact.

I’ve spent most of my career in design. At first writing as little as possible. But I have learnt that writing is a skill required within the creative industry. One that I’ve taken on with open arms. Whether you’re an Art Director, Graphic Designer or Account Manager, you should too. And yes, it is a skill. Being able to write well greatly improves the potential for success. Whatever the goal of writing is. But it’s a skill that can be developed regardless of background or core skills.

Having learned the hard way, though, I've been taking more and more notice of what makes good writing. The styles, the techniques, the rules. And what I’ve discovered is a pattern. What distinguishes good writing from bad writing is a set of traits that writers follow. Regardless of industry or writing style or tone.

One tense for one piece of writing.

You will write for many different purposes in your time as a designer. So there’ll be times where writing in a different tense is necessary. There are no real rules in using a present tense over past tense. But it’s always one or the other. Never both within the same piece of writing. Mixing both within an article is bad; you'll confuse the reader. Your writing will also become weaker. Know who’s telling the story.
